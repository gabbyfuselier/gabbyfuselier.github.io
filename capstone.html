Pace

Overview

Role: Researcher, Designer, Engineer
Tools: Arduino IDE, microcontroller, vibromotor, capacitive touch sensor
Project Type: Physical design, product design, physical computing

Pace began as a habit-tracking robot, but quickly evolved into something more nuanced: a physical tool for nervous system regulation. Instead of focusing on productivity or reward systems, the project shifted toward supporting awareness, grounding, and behavioral redirection through tactile interaction.

Context

This project originated as a capstone exploring habit formation and physical interaction design. Early concepts focused on gamified habit tracking but in the real world, bringing a habit tracking helper into the physical world. This was then shifted when I realized the limitations of this project and that the changes that would need to be made to make it work no longer brought me joy.

Key shift

I was asked by my professor to come up with alternative options and through some soul searching, and much help from her, I came up with this:

“Instead of perpetuating anxious behaviors, what if I made something that redirects them.”

This shifted Pace from a “habit tracker” into a tactile regulation tool, something that guides behavior subtly rather than tracking or judging it.

User experience flow

So I started by redesigning the experience. Focusing on meeting users where they are, not forcing calm immediately.

1. User reaches for the object during stress
2. Fidgeting begins naturally
3. Breathing pattern runs in parallel
4. User may begin to mirror rhythm
5. Movement slows
6. Transition into stillness and breath

This creates a gradual shift from energy release to regulation.

Research & exploration

I started by getting to know the types of fidgets a user might want. I designed and 3D printed a fidget box with many different types of fidgets. With the overwhelming consensus being: movement is key. Slow movement improves physical anxiety better than free form movement, so that is where my focus went.

I then designed the first iteration of the form. Trying to keep the "buddy" aspect of the original idea, the first form was a simplified dinosaur. I gave this form to friends and family and allowed them to hold it, mess around with it, and get used to it. Afterward, asking them what kind of fidget they believed it should be and if they liked the form. Two ideas were prominent amongst the masses: the head should spin, and they should be able to rub the belly. As for the actual form, it was too short and stubby. The legs made it hard to hold and the tail was too long and sharp.

First redesign

This feedback lead to the first major redesign. I moved the legs to a new position changing the orientation and size the make it easier to hold. I also added in the first worry stone to the belly and took out a slot for the chestplat to move in and out of.

First challenges

With progress there are always set backs. The first I encountered being issues with 3D printing. Where my first print did not have enought filament and the sensor was broken so it did not stop printing when it ran out. The second issue being problems with the tension of the belt on the printer causing it to move at odd angles and break pieces off of my print.

Technical issues

Key challenges included:

Fitting battery, motor, and components into the form
Calibrating motion speed for natural breathing
Making touch sensors work through material thickness
Managing friction and mechanical resistance

Multiple iterations involved resizing, reconfiguring internal layouts, and testing alternative materials like copper tape for touch sensing.

Failures—such as prints not fitting, motors not performing correctly, and sensors not detecting reliably—directly informed each redesign.
