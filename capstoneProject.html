Pace

Overview

Role: Researcher, Designer, Engineer
Tools: Arduino IDE, microcontroller, vibromotor, capacitive touch sensor
Project Type: Physical design, product design, physical computing

Pace began as a habit-tracking robot, but quickly evolved into something more nuanced: a physical tool for nervous system regulation. Instead of focusing on productivity or reward systems, the project shifted toward supporting awareness, grounding, and behavioral redirection through tactile interaction.

Context

This project originated as a capstone exploring habit formation and physical interaction design. Early concepts focused on gamified habit tracking, but user feedback and research led to a pivot toward calmer, non-judgmental feedback and embodied interaction.

Key Shift

Most habit tools focus on achievement, streaks, or reward. Through research and testing, I reframed the project:

“Positive feedback should reinforce nervous system regulation, not achievement.”

This shifted Bodi from a “habit tracker” into a tactile regulation tool—something that guides behavior subtly rather than tracking or judging it.

Research & Exploration

Initial exploration focused on identifying patterns users may want to become more aware of, rather than strictly “good” or “bad” habits.

These included:

Positive patterns: drinking water, taking breaks, deep breaths, setting boundaries
Negative patterns: nail biting, phone overuse, rumination, avoidance

A key insight emerged:

Tracking too many behaviors becomes overwhelming.
Focusing on one pattern at a time creates a more meaningful and sustainable interaction.

Designing Feedback

Rather than using gamification or rewards, feedback was designed to regulate the body.

Key feedback types included:

Physiological calming: slow breathing motion, gentle vibration, warmth
Grounding: weight shifts, texture awareness
Emotional mirroring: relaxed posture, stillness
Temporal closure: short responses that return to neutral

The goal was not excitement, but a feeling of:

“That helped.”

Fidget as Regulation

Traditional fidget tools release energy but don’t guide behavior. This project explored how tactile interaction could actively support regulation.

Instead of stopping anxious behaviors, Bodi redirects them.

Design features included:

Pickable edges
Soft seams
Raised textures
Stretchable elements
Pressable surfaces

This allows the device to act as a replacement behavior rather than a restriction.

Iteration & Testing

Form + Tactile Testing

Early fidget prototypes revealed that sensation alone wasn’t enough. Some textures were distracting or uncomfortable, while others were calming but lacked intention.

This led to refining: - scale and spacing of textures - depth of interaction surfaces - overall ergonomics

Reframing Fidgets

A major insight:

Most fidgets are designed for energy release, not regulation.

This project instead focused on:

slow rhythm
resistance
predictable motion
guided interaction

Form Development

User feedback highlighted the importance of intuitive handling. Adjustments included:

Clear grip positioning
Reducing bulk
Improving access to interaction zones
Stabilizing the form

Technical Development

The internal system required balancing size constraints with functionality.

Key challenges included:

Fitting battery, motor, and components into the form
Calibrating motion speed for natural breathing
Making touch sensors work through material thickness
Managing friction and mechanical resistance

Multiple iterations involved resizing, reconfiguring internal layouts, and testing alternative materials like copper tape for touch sensing.

Failures—such as prints not fitting, motors not performing correctly, and sensors not detecting reliably—directly informed each redesign.

User Experience Flow

The experience was designed to meet users where they are, not force calm immediately.

1. User reaches for the object during stress
2. Fidgeting begins naturally
3. Breathing pattern runs in parallel
4. User may begin to mirror rhythm
5. Movement slows
6. Transition into stillness and breath

This creates a gradual shift from energy release to regulation.

Outcome

The final direction positions Bodi as:

A tactile regulation companion, not a productivity tool.

It supports awareness, redirects behavior, and reinforces calm without judgment, tracking, or pressure.

Reflection

This process shifted my understanding of behavior design. What began as a habit tracker became an exploration of how physical interaction can support mental regulation.

I learned that:

Designing for calm requires restraint, not stimulation
Feedback should support the body, not reward performance
Iteration is driven as much by failure as success

The most important takeaway was learning how to design experiences that feel supportive rather than controlling—subtle enough to integrate into daily life, but intentional enough to make an impact.
